News from the Research Group

October 10, 2025
A new paper published
We are pleased to announce that our latest research paper has been published in the Journal of High Energy Physics.

A central challenge in numerical studies of quantum field theories is ensuring that simulations correctly reproduce the thermodynamic properties of the underlying system. In this work, we introduce a new way to directly measure temperature in lattice gauge theory simulations, using only information from the field configurations themselves.

The proposed temperature estimator is built from derivatives of the Euclidean action—specifically its gradients and curvature—and does not rely on kinetic energy or auxiliary variables. This makes it naturally gauge-invariant and well suited as a diagnostic tool for checking thermodynamic consistency in Monte Carlo simulations. The approach is inspired by geometric ideas from classical statistical mechanics, adapted to the lattice field theory setting.

We test the method in compact U(1) lattice gauge theories in one, two, and four dimensions, comparing the measured temperature with the temperature set by the temporal extent of the lattice. Across a wide range of lattice sizes and coupling strengths, the estimator accurately reproduces the expected temperature, demonstrating both robustness and reliability.

Beyond these benchmark tests, the estimator offers a general-purpose diagnostic with broad potential applications. These include extensions to non-Abelian gauge theories, anisotropic lattices, and real-time monitoring of thermalization and stability in hybrid Monte Carlo algorithms.

The published article is available in Journal of High Energy Physics, Vol. 2025, Article 15 (2025) , and an open-access preprint can be found on arXiv:2508.05595.
June 10, 2025
A new review article published
We are happy to share that our comprehensive review of the Complex Langevin Method (CLM) has been published in the International Journal of Modern Physics A.

Many of the most interesting problems in quantum field theory and particle physics are extremely difficult to study numerically because of the so-called sign problem. The Complex Langevin Method offers an alternative approach, using stochastic (random) processes rather than standard probability sampling to explore theories with complex actions.

In this review, we provide an accessible overview of how CLM works, starting from its basic principles and building up to modern applications. We discuss its use in a wide range of models, from simple low-dimensional systems to supersymmetric quantum mechanics and the IKKT matrix model, a prominent candidate for a nonperturbative formulation of string theory.

A central theme of the review is the progress made in overcoming the practical challenges of the method. We describe recent stabilization techniques and deformation strategies that help control numerical instabilities and problematic drift behavior, making reliable simulations possible in increasingly complex settings.

Beyond surveying existing results, the article also looks ahead. We discuss open challenges and future directions, including extensions to higher-dimensional theories and systematic comparisons with emerging quantum-simulation approaches. Together, these developments position the Complex Langevin Method as a powerful and evolving tool for exploring nonperturbative physics in quantum field theory and string theory.

The published article is available in International Journal of Modern Physics A, Vol. 40, No. 20 (2025) 2530006 , and an open-access preprint can be found on arXiv:2504.02660.
May 17, 2025
A new paper published
We are pleased to announce that our research paper, “Finite-temperature phase diagram of the Berenstein–Maldacena–Nastase matrix model on the lattice,” has been published in Physical Review D.

Understanding how quantum systems behave at finite temperature is a central challenge in modern theoretical physics, with connections ranging from particle physics to quantum gravity. In this work, we study the thermal properties of a simplified but highly informative model known as the Berenstein–Maldacena–Nastase (BMN) matrix model, which plays an important role in string theory and gauge–gravity duality.

Using large-scale numerical simulations on a spacetime lattice, we explore how the model changes as the temperature and interaction strength are varied. Our calculations span a wide range of parameters, allowing us to smoothly connect regimes where traditional analytical methods work to those where they break down. In particular, we determine the temperature at which the system undergoes a transition between different phases and show how this transition evolves from weak to strong coupling.

One of our key findings is that the nature of the phase transition itself changes: it is abrupt at strong coupling, while becoming smooth and continuous at weaker coupling. These results provide a nonperturbative bridge between known theoretical predictions and strongly interacting regimes that are otherwise difficult to access.

The published article is available in Physical Review D 111 (2025) 094516, and an open-access preprint can be found on arXiv:2412.13407.
